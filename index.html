I am a social data scientist studying electoral corruption, fraud, and the bureaucracy. I analyze large, complex administrative data, including granular polling station results, censuses, voter registers, and public budgets. In collaboration with unions and non-for-profits, I have designed and implemented large-scale field experiments in Denmark to study public satisfaction with the bureaucracy and crime misperceptions.

In my work I use a range of supervised machine learning models to classify observations, fx with respect to local representation in parliamentary speeches; the partisanship of radio stations; and respondents' preferred economic solution to COVID in open-text format. I am a keen user of R stats. I have taught courses on statistics and programming in addition to substantive courses on comparative politics to undergraduate and graduates and have supervised masters theses.

About me

I am a postdoctoral fellow at the University of Copenhagen. I hold a PhD and an MSc in political science from the University of Copenhagen as well as an MSc in comparative politics from the London School of Economics and Political Science. In 2019 I was a Fulbright visiting fellow at Harvard University's Institute for Quantitative Social Science and I was a visiting scholar at The Center on the Politics of Development, UC Berkeley. I am currently employed as a postdoctoral fellow at the University of Copenhagen and part of the Behavioral and Experimental Public Administration Lab (BEPAL). I previously held a position as project scientist at Uber Denmark.
