Around the world, electoral playing fields are skewed towards the incumbent regime. My research describes how the electoral playing is skewed by combining the study of clientelism and the study of institutional manipulation. Specifically, I study the electoral infrastructure - the institutions in place to organize elections - and ask questions like: - What preferences do authoritarian regimes have over different electoral systems? - Do authoritarian regimes have in interest in increasing or decreasing the electorate's ease of voting? - Does the electoral infrastructure shape the clientelistic strategies of parties in power? In a different strand of work, I am engaged with how to improve public sector accountability through the provision of performance information to citizens using field-experiments.

About me

I hold a PhD and an MSc in political science from the University of Copenhagen as well as an MSc in comparative politics from the London School of Economics and Political Science. In 2019 I was a Fulbright visiting scholar at Harvard University's Institute for Quantitative Social Science. I am currently employed as a postdoctoral fellow at the University of Copenhagen.
